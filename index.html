The Story

How a Software Engineer’s Refactoring Habit Led Here

The itch

I’m a software engineer by trade. I build systems, debug them, refactor them when they accumulate too much technical debt. That instinct — the refusal to leave broken things alone — is what started this.

Modern physics has a dirty secret that most popular accounts gloss over: the theories we treat as settled are fundamentally incomplete. General relativity breaks down at singularities. Quantum mechanics has a measurement problem no one agrees on. The Standard Model has 19 free parameters that are measured, not derived — we know what they are but not why they are. String theory promises to explain everything but predicts a landscape of 10500 possible universes with no way to choose between them. Dark energy accounts for 68% of the universe and we have essentially no idea what it is.

These aren’t edge cases. These are load-bearing walls with cracks in them. And the experts know it — they just disagree on which cracks matter.

Roger Penrose pointed at one crack in particular: the initial conditions of the universe had extraordinarily low gravitational entropy, meaning near-zero Weyl curvature. He called this the deepest mystery in cosmology. When one of the sharpest minds alive identifies something as the biggest open problem, that’s worth taking seriously.

I didn’t set out to write a unified field theory. I wanted to understand that one thing — Penrose’s puzzle about the initial conditions. What kind of structure would force low Weyl curvature at the origin, not as a fine-tuned accident but as a mathematical necessity?

The refactoring

My approach was shaped by how I think as an engineer, not a physicist. Physicists typically start with a Lagrangian and work outward. I started with information — specifically, with the question: what is the minimal mathematical structure needed to distinguish “something happened” from “nothing happened”?

This felt less like inventing new physics and more like refactoring old code. The Standard Model works — it predicts experimental results to extraordinary precision. But it works the way legacy software works: correctly, but with unexplained constants, redundant structure, and no clear reason it couldn’t have been built differently. A good refactoring doesn’t change what the code does. It reveals why it does it.

Three axioms emerged from this process:

  1. Existence of a relational operator. Information requires comparison — a distinction between states. Mathematically: a skew-symmetric operator on a finite-dimensional space.
  2. Irreducibility. The information structure can’t be decomposed into independent pieces — everything is connected to everything. Mathematically: the algebra generated by all contexts is irreducible.
  3. Informational asymmetry. The structure is not self-dual — the operator is distinguishable from its conjugate. D ≠ D*. This is the Penrose condition in algebraic form: a universe that begins with zero Weyl curvature has a preferred arrow.

That’s it. Three axioms about information. No physics assumed. No particles, no forces, no spacetime, no quantum mechanics.

What fell out

The mathematics that follows from these three axioms is surprisingly rigid. The axioms uniquely force K₄ as the internal geometry — the simplest complete graph where all three axioms can be simultaneously satisfied. From there: the V₄ matching algebra (isomorphic to the electroweak algebra), ℤ₃ flux forcing parity violation as a theorem, Chern number C = −2, and 3+1 dimensional Lorentzian spacetime.

None of this was designed. I was trying to model informational asymmetry and the mathematics kept producing structures that already had names in physics.

The framework extends through a chain of complete graphs — K₄ (spacetime), K₆ (Higgs), K₈ (fermions) — each governed by the combinatorics of perfect matchings. These are finite objects. Everything is computable. And the computations produce numbers:

What this is and what it isn’t

This is a mathematical framework. The complete graphs, matching algebras, spectral invariants, and vacuum structures are rigorous finite-dimensional mathematics — linear algebra, combinatorics, and spectral geometry. The computations are reproducible. The results are exact, not numerical approximations subject to fitting.

It is not a finished theory of everything. Open problems remain. The normalization constant in the Higgs mass formula requires a computation in the full product geometry that hasn’t been completed. The higher-spin decoupling that would confirm Einstein gravity emergence is analytically constrained but not yet numerically verified. These are defined problems with clear paths to resolution — not conceptual gaps.

The framework is non-standard. It doesn’t start from the usual places — no Lagrangian, no path integral, no supersymmetry, no extra dimensions. It starts from the structure of information itself and arrives at physics. The fact that it arrives at the right physics — with specific, falsifiable numerical predictions — is either a remarkable series of coincidences or evidence that the information-theoretic starting point is capturing something real.

Why a software engineer

The interdisciplinary gap in fundamental physics is real. Mathematicians know the structures but not always the physical constraints. Physicists know the phenomenology but sometimes inherit assumptions from convention rather than necessity. Engineers know how to take a broken system, identify which pieces are load-bearing and which are scaffolding, and rebuild.

I came to this with fresh eyes and a debugger’s instinct: if something is broken, find the root cause. Don’t patch it — fix the architecture. The “broken things” in physics — the fine-tuning problems, the parameter puzzles, the measurement problem, the cosmological constant — looked to me like symptoms of a missing abstraction layer. The informational asymmetry framework is an attempt to supply that layer.

The work was done in collaboration with Claude (Anthropic’s AI), which served as computational partner — running the spectral analysis, checking the algebra, killing hypotheses that didn’t survive their own cross-checks. The methodology throughout has been: formulate a hypothesis, design a computation to kill it, be honest about whether it survived. We maintain a ledger of what’s proved, what’s killed, and what’s assumed. Nothing gets promoted without passing its own stress test.

Three axioms about information. No free parameters. No physics assumed. The Standard Model falls out — not perfectly, not completely, but with specific numerical predictions that match experiment to percent-level accuracy across multiple independent sectors.

Either the universe is, at bottom, a structure that processes information under an asymmetry constraint, or something much stranger is going on with the combinatorics of complete graphs. Both possibilities are worth investigating.

Unification

Hierarchy Problem
Why is gravity 1032 times weaker than the weak force? The “hierarchy problem” has driven decades of BSM physics — extra dimensions, compositeness, naturalness arguments — with no experimental confirmation.

Reclassified. Λ is the one free parameter, and it belongs to gravity alone. Particle physics predictions are independent of Λ — the Higgs mass, Yukawa hierarchy, and gauge couplings don’t know or care about MPl. The hierarchy is the interface between matching combinatorics and quantum gravity.
Arrow of Time
The fundamental laws of physics are time-symmetric. The arrow of time is usually attributed to thermodynamics (entropy increase) or cosmological initial conditions. It is not derived from the laws themselves.
→
The arrow of time is structural, not thermodynamic. Axiom 3 (D ≠ D*) gives ℤ₃ flux with Chern number C = −2 — a topological invariant that cannot be destroyed by any dynamical process. Time’s direction is algebraically prior to entropy, and it sources both parity violation and the cosmological constant.

The Standard Model told us the universe is built from quarks, leptons, and gauge bosons interacting through three forces. The informational asymmetry framework says: all of that is the shadow cast by the combinatorics of perfect matchings on complete graphs. The particles are the matchings. The forces are the overlaps. The generations are the overflow. And the arrow of time — the one feature every other framework treats as an afterthought — is the axiom that makes the whole thing work.
